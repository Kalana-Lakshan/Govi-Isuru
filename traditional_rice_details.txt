1) Samba Rice
Description:
- Short, ovular grain with a slightly plump shape. Popular for daily meals and special dishes. Occurs in both white and red-seeded forms. Many local landraces are aromatic and have good cooking quality, often slightly sticky when cooked.

Common diseases & pests:
- Rice blast (Magnaporthe oryzae)
- Bacterial leaf blight (Xanthomonas oryzae pv. oryzae)
- Sheath blight (Rhizoctonia solani)
- Brown spot (Bipolaris oryzae)
- Brown planthopper (Nilaparvata lugens) and associated virus or hopperburn
- Stem borers and rice hispa

Prevention & management:
- Use certified, disease-resistant Samba cultivars where available.
- Practice crop rotation and field sanitation (remove volunteer rice, stubble burning or incorporation as local practice allows).
- Maintain proper plant spacing and avoid excessive nitrogen which increases susceptibility to blast and planthoppers.
- Seed treatment with approved fungicide and insecticidal seed dressings reduces early infections.
- Timely scouting; apply fungicides or insecticides only when economic thresholds are met (follow local DOA guidance).
- Balanced irrigation managementâ€”avoid prolonged leaf wetness when blast risk is high.

Nutrition & fertilization (general guidance):
- Conduct soil test to set targets. For typical smallholder Samba: aim for balanced N-P-K with attention to organic matter.
- Basal: Apply full P and K according to soil test at or before transplanting/sowing; incorporate organic matter (compost) where possible.
- Nitrogen split: Common schedule â€” 1/3 basal at planting, 1/3 at active tillering (20â€“30 days after transplanting), 1/3 at panicle initiation (just before heading). Adjust rates to yield target (e.g., total N 60â€“120 kg/ha depending on soil fertility and desired yield).
- Micronutrients: Apply zinc if deficiency suspected (ZnSO4 at planting) and ensure sulphur and boron levels are adequate for panicle health.

Harvesting & post-harvest:
- Harvest when ~85â€“90% of grains on the main tillers are firm and straw has started to yellow; typical grain moisture at cutting ~20â€“24%.
- Cut and thresh promptly; dry grain to safe storage moisture (12â€“14%) to avoid spoilage.
- For best quality, avoid shattering by harvesting before overmaturity; red-seeded traditional Sambas may require gentler handling to preserve bran color.


2) Kekulu Rice
Description:
- Term `Kekulu` broadly refers to small to medium-grained traditional varieties similar in some ways to Nadu; often consumed as daily white or red rice. May be non-parboiled and has a tender texture when cooked.

Common diseases & pests:
- Similar to general rice: blast, bacterial leaf blight, sheath blight, brown spot, stem borer, planthoppers.

Prevention & management:
- Use clean seed; practice crop/habitat hygiene.
- Avoid dense planting; follow recommended seed rates.
- Integrated pest management: natural enemies, pheromone traps for borers, and selective insecticides as needed.

Nutrition & fertilization:
- Follow soil-test recommendations. Traditional Kekulu types respond well to organic amendments.
- Typical N split: 1/3 basal, 1/3 at tillering, 1/3 at panicle initiation.
- Apply phosphorus at planting to promote root establishment; potassium to support grain filling.

Harvesting & post-harvest:
- Harvest when grains mature but before significant shattering; drying to 12â€“14% moisture for storage.
- For non-parboiled Kekulu, careful drying and storage prevents rancidity of the bran.


3) Keeri Samba / Supiri Samba
Description:
- Keeri Samba (often called Keeri Samba or Supiri Samba) is a premium small-grain aromatic rice valued for its flavor and distinct texture. Grains are small, somewhat ovate, and often used in special dishes and traditional preparations.

Common diseases & pests:
- Same disease spectrum: blast, sheath blight, bacterial blight, brown spot; aromatic landraces can be more susceptible to lodging if overfertilized.
- Insect pests: stem borers, leaf folders, planthoppers.

Prevention & management:
- Grow in well-drained, fertile soils but avoid excessive N to preserve aroma and reduce lodging and pest attraction.
- Prefer gap-filling rather than heavy reseeding to maintain plant health.
- Use IPM: pheromone traps, biological controls, and spot treatments rather than broad sprays to protect beneficials that maintain aroma quality.

Nutrition & fertilization:
- Lower to moderate nitrogen rates often produce best cooking quality and aroma; example split N at lower end of recommended range (60â€“80 kg N/ha) unless soil is poor.
- Ensure phosphorus for root development and potassium for grain quality.
- Topdress N at tillering and panicle initiation; avoid late excessive N after heading (can reduce grain quality).

Harvesting & post-harvest:
- For aromatic varieties, harvest at physiological maturity (grain hard, panicle slightly drooping) to maximize aroma and reduce losses.
- Dry carefully at low temperatures and avoid overheating during parboiling or drying to preserve aroma compounds.


4) Suwandel / Suwandal
Description:
- Suwandel (also spelled Suwandal or Suwandel) is a fragrant, traditional Sri Lankan rice often used in festive and ceremonial dishes. It is prized for aroma and slightly distinct taste/texture compared with common varieties.

Common diseases & pests:
- Vulnerable to the usual rice diseases: blast, sheath blight, brown spot, bacterial blight, and insect pests like planthoppers and borers.

Prevention & management:
- Use certified or locally adapted Suwandel seed; stagger plantings to avoid peak pest pressure.
- Maintain field hygiene and use appropriate resistant varieties if available.
- Rotational legumes and green-manure crops reduce disease carryover and improve soil nitrogen.

Nutrition & fertilization:
- Suwandel benefits from balanced nutrition. Maintain good soil organic matter for aroma expression.
- Typical N schedule: split application (basal, tillering, panicle initiation); avoid late N.
- Apply K for grain filling; consider foliar micro-nutrients when deficiency symptoms appear.

Harvesting & post-harvest:
- Harvest at physiological maturity; dry to safe moisture quickly to retain aroma.
- Careful threshing and minimal grain damage preserve market value.


5) Nadu Rice
Description:
- Nadu is a medium-grain rice type widely grown and consumed in Sri Lanka. It often refers to grain size/shape rather than a single cultivar group; available in white and red variants and used for daily meals.

Common diseases & pests:
- Rice blast, sheath blight, bacterial leaf blight, brown spot, tungro virus (transmitted by leafhoppers), planthoppers, stem borers.

Prevention & management:
- Use recommended varieties and certified seed.
- Control vector populations (leafhoppers) to reduce tungro risk â€” maintain healthy, vigorous plants and consider insecticidal seed treatment.
- Balanced fertilization and appropriate water management reduce disease incidence.

Nutrition & fertilization:
- Nadu is responsive to N but excessive N increases disease and pest risks.
- Standard split N program: basal, tillering, panicle initiation â€” adjust total N to soil fertility and yield goal (often 60â€“120 kg N/ha range as an example).
- Phosphorus at planting, potassium in early/mid-season for grain filling.

Harvesting & post-harvest:
- Harvest when grains on main tillers are mature (85â€“90% grain maturity), typically indicated by firm grains and yellowing straw.
- Thresh and dry to 12â€“14% moisture for storage; reduce field losses by timely harvesting to prevent shattering and bird/pest damage.


General Best Practices (applies to all traditional varieties):
- Seed selection: Use clean, certified seed when available. Clean seed reduces disease carryover and improves stand uniformity.
- Land prep & planting: Ensure good seedbed and leveled fields for uniform irrigation. Follow recommended seed rates and planting spacing to reduce disease microclimates.
- Water management: Alternate wetting and drying or maintain recommended water depth depending on local recommendations; avoid prolonged leaf wetness when fungal diseases are prevalent.
- Integrated Pest Management (IPM): Combine cultural controls (rotation, sanitation), biologicals (natural enemies), and targeted chemical interventions only as needed.
- Fertility: Perform soil tests. Use organic matter (compost, green manure) to improve soil health and micronutrient availability. Base fertilizer programs on soil-test results; follow split N applications timed to tillering and panicle initiation.
- Harvest timing: Harvest at physiological maturity to balance yield and quality; drying to safe moisture quickly is essential to preserve grain quality and prevent pests/molds.
- Storage: Store dry grain at 12â€“14% moisture in cool, dry conditions; protect against insects and rodents with cleanliness and sealed containers or appropriate storage structures.

- The above compendium is based on standard rice agronomy and integrated disease/pest management practices as taught by IRRI, FAO, and national extension services. H 9

Description:

Major Diseases:

Blast disease

Sheath blight

Disease Prevention:

Use certified disease-free seeds

Maintain proper water management

Avoid excessive nitrogen fertilizer

Apply recommended fungicides when symptoms appear

Harvesting:
Harvest when 80â€“85% of grains are mature and straw turns yellow. Cut plants close to the ground and dry grains properly before storage.

Bg 3-5

Description:
Bg 3-5 is a high-yielding improved variety with long growth duration. It has good adaptability to different environments and produces medium-sized grains.

Major Diseases:

Blast

Brown spot

Bacterial leaf blight

Disease Prevention:

Practice crop rotation

Use balanced fertilizer application

Ensure proper field drainage

Apply fungicides if disease incidence is high

Harvesting:
Harvest at full maturity when grains are hard and panicles bend downward. Timely harvesting prevents grain shattering.

Bg 38

Description:
Bg 38 is a traditional long-duration rice variety known for its tolerance to varied soil conditions. It has moderate yield with good grain quality.

Major Diseases:

Blast

Brown spot

Maintain proper spacing between plants

Avoid water stagnation for long periods

Apply recommended plant protection chemicals

Harvesting:
Harvest when grains reach physiological maturity. Dry harvested panicles under sunlight to reduce moisture content.

Bg 407

Description:

Major Diseases:

Blast

Bacterial leaf blight

Bg 745

Description:

Major Diseases:

Blast

Follow integrated pest and disease management practices

How these codes relate to the earlier sections:
- Codes beginning with `Bg`, `At`, `Bw`, `Ld`, `H`, `MI` are released or tested breeding lines and recommended varieties; many correspond to high-yield modern varieties or improved lines. Traditional types like `Samba`, `Kekulu`, `Nadu`, `Keeri Samba`, and `Suwandel` are landrace/traditional categories discussed earlier and may not map one-to-one to these codes.
- Use the above codes to look up official release information (maturity days, yield potential, recommended fertilizer rates, and disease resistance) from the Department of Agriculture or variety release bulletins.



Additional Traditional Varieties (extracted from Wikipedia & Lakpura pages)

Suwandel / Suwandal

Description: Fragrant white heirloom rice with a strong natural aroma; widely used for festive, ceremonial, and special dishes.

Nutrients: ~90% carbohydrates, ~7% protein, ~0.7% fat, ~0.1% fiber; relatively higher glutamic acid and some vitamins.

Typical maturity: ~3 months (varies by line and season).

Major diseases: Blast, brown spot, bacterial leaf blight.

Disease prevention: Use healthy traditional seed stock; avoid excessive nitrogen; maintain good drainage; apply fungicide only if disease is severe.

Harvesting: Harvest when panicles turn golden yellow and grains are hard. Cut plants early morning and sun-dry grains properly.

Typical maturity: ~3.5â€“4 months.

Harvesting: Harvest at 85% grain maturity; delay may cause lodging due to tall plants.

Maa-Wee (Ma-Wee)

Description: Reddish-brown rice with lower carbohydrate and higher protein and fiber than common rice.

Major diseases: Brown spot, blast, sheath rot.

Disease prevention: Crop rotation; organic matter incorporation; avoid stagnant water.

Harvesting: Harvest when grains are fully filled; soaking straw before threshing reduces grain loss.
